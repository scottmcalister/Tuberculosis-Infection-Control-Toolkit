Fit Testing

Fit testing is a necessary component of a respiratory protection program. Fit testing is required to identify the appropriate model and size respirator for each HCW to ensure that the respirator fits properly and securely and does not impair breathing or obstruct the wearer’s sight. All respirators that rely on a mask-to-face seal must be fit tested to validate the seal is air-tight. Fit testing should be done:

• prior to first issue,
• when there's a change in the model, style or size of respirator used,
• when there's a physical facial change in the person wearing the respirator, and
• at least annually

Fit testing should be repeated on an annual basis, as there might be changes in the HCW’s physical condition that can affect the respirator’s fit (e.g., changes in weight, dental work, facial scarring, etc.). There is no licensed certification required for someone to conduct fit testing – any individual can do so as long as they understand how to follow the protocol and are skilled at training people on how to perform a user seal check. However, it is the RPA’s responsibility to ensure that the person conducting fit testing is competent, and that this individual follows the protocol as written in the standard.

There are two approved methods for respirator fit testing: quantitative fit testing and qualitative fit testing:

1. There are three types of quantitative fit tests, all of which and all require relatively expensive equipment, and provide an objective assessment of the respirator’s efficacy, eliminating any subjectivity that a qualitative test provides. Thus for lower-resource settings, qualitative fit testing might be a more preferable option. The most common quantitative protocol is the ambient aerosol condensation nuclei counter (CNC) test, which provides an automatic calculation of the effectiveness fit or “fit factor” – the ratio of the concentration of microscopic particles in the ambient air to the concentration particles that leak into the respirator. The required fit factor for passing the test depends on the type of respirator used. Unlike a qualitative fit test, quantitative fit tests are not affected by the person's sense of smell, taste or sensitivity to irritant chemicals.
2. A qualitiative fit test is a pass/fail test that relies on the HCWs subjective sensory (taste, irritation, smell) response to a particular test agent, typically —saccharin (sweet-tasting) or Bitrex® (bitter-tasting) test aerosols. The qualitative fit test agent uses a person’s response to a sweet taste to detect leakage into the respirator while performing a series of exercises that simulate normal movements under possible work conditions. Because these tests rely on the user’s subjective detection of leakage when challenged with a test agent, the HCW must be pre-screened to determine his/her ability to detect the specific test agent.
